People assume the dream is a restaurant. We're starting with a truck on purpose, and the reasons are all numbers.

Start with margin. Owner-run food trucks tend to land somewhere around 6 to 15 percent net — call those industry estimates, not gospel — while sit-down restaurants often run closer to 3 to 5. The startup gap is bigger still: a truck costs a fraction of a brick-and-mortar build-out. And a truck moves. It goes to where the crowd already is instead of praying the crowd finds your door.

Here's the part that sounds like bad news and is actually the opening. The food-truck gold rush is over. The count has flattened. The easy-money rush priced itself out, and what's left is a field where operator quality decides who wins — not novelty, not being first. That's the gap we're walking into. When everyone was piling in, showing the math was a disadvantage. Now it's the moat.

**When the rush ends, the operator who shows the math wins.**

Then there's the food itself. Local sourcing was the number-one restaurant trend of 2025, and we happen to be standing in farm country. Spokane sits inside it — Green Bluff orchards, the Palouse, grass-fed ranches, LINC Foods moving local product. Most trucks can't credibly say "we sourced this here." We can, and we can show exactly where.

The boring operational facts are where most people quit, so here they are. The permit order matters: Washington L&I comes before the health permit, not after. You return to a commissary every single day — that's not optional, it's the law. The gray-water tank has to be roughly 15 percent larger than the fresh-water tank, because everything that goes in has to come back out. Budget lands around $85k all in. None of that is glamorous. All of it is real, and getting it wrong is how trucks die before they sell a thing.

Honest caveat: Spokane isn't on anyone's "best food city" list. We know. It's a growing scene, not a finished one — and that's the bet, not a thing we're hiding. We'd rather plant the flag while the ground is still open than fight for a corner of a city that's already decided who its winners are.

A truck is the cheapest way to prove the food, the operator, and the audience all at once. So that's the first rung.

We don't pitch. We prove.

Own your slice.
